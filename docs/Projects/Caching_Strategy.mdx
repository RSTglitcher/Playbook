**Project Purpose**  
To assess and align on a caching strategy that improves service resilience and availability under high‑traffic conditions, while balancing complexity, cost, and delivery feasibility across platform teams.

**Problem Statement**  
High‑traffic “binge” events exposed database throttling and availability risks, but it was unclear whether additional caching (local or distributed) would meaningfully improve outcomes or introduce unnecessary architectural complexity.

**Value Added:**
- Separated concerns between BAU vs binge traffic, local vs distributed cache, and incident mitigation vs long-term platform strategy
- Prevented premature architectural decisions by separating incident response from platform requirements
- Translated incidents and friction into Explicit questions and trade-off analysis, Alignment workshops with clear outcomes,  Post-meeting notes with action items
- Used observed hit/miss ratios, pod counts, and traffic locality to inform infrastructure decisions

## 🧭 **Context & Background**

This work was undertaken during a Boomerang placement within the GPE Core Services / Lakitu ecosystem, following incidents triggered by traffic spikes.

- **Trigger / Catalyst**  
  A customer‑facing outage during a high‑demand event (e.g. NFL binge traffic) revealed read‑throttling limits in downstream databases.

- **Relevant History**  
  - Initial proposals focused on adding additional caching layers.  
  - Discussion quickly broadened into whether a **local cache** or a **shared distributed cache** (e.g. Redis/Elasticache) was appropriate.  
  - Multiple teams held different definitions of “simple” and “low risk”.

- **Domain Boundaries Involved**  
  Video, TM, SSE, platform and performance engineering teams.

- **Assumptions Already in Place**  
  - Caching should protect the database as a last line of defence.  
  - Not all traffic patterns benefit equally from caching.

- **Known Constraints**  
  - Existing pod scaling and request patterns  
  - Operational overhead of new infrastructure

## 🔍 **Analysis Plan**

### **Key Questions to Resolve**

- What traffic patterns actually justify caching?
- Does cross‑pod cache sharing provide measurable benefit?
- Is the added complexity of distributed caching justified?
- What is the lowest‑risk improvement that meaningfully improves resilience?

### **Investigation Steps**

- Review Grafana data to understand request locality and hit/miss ratios  
- Compare BAU vs binge behaviour across services  
- Assess pod counts and database load characteristics  
- Document pros/cons of local vs distributed cache options  
- Facilitate a workshop to align stakeholders

### **Artefacts Produced**

- Caching strategy documentation  
- Behaviour analysis summaries  
- Trade‑off tables  
- Alignment workshop outputs  

---

## 🧩 **Architecture & Design Notes**

- **Communication Pattern Choices:**  
  Workshop‑driven alignment rather than unilateral recommendation

- **Storage Decisions:**  
  Preference for per‑pod local caches where behaviour supports it

- **Failure Modes Considered:**  
  - Cache stampede  
  - Cold‑start behaviour during deployments  
  - Increased operational overhead

- **Trade‑offs Considered:**  
  Simplicity and delivery speed vs long‑term scalability

- **Alternatives Rejected (and why):**  
  Distributed caching introduced cost and complexity without clear benefit given observed traffic patterns.
