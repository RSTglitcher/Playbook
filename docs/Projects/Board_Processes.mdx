:::tip 📌 Executive Summary


**Project Purpose**  
To transform the Department Tech Backlog from a passive holding list into a formalised, decision‑ready board that supports 
prioritisation, accountability, and predictable delivery of engineering‑led work, including technical debt.


**Problem Statement**  
The Tech Backlog is not a performative board: it accumulates work without clear intake criteria, ownership, or commitment 
to delivery, and ceases to function during periods of constrained capacity, reducing visibility of technical risk and 
undermining planning and predictability.
:::

## 🧭 **Context & Background**


This work was initiated in response to growing concerns about the effectiveness of the Department Tech Backlog and its 
ability to support engineering planning.


- **Trigger / Catalyst**  
  Persistent growth and ageing of the Tech Backlog with little evidence of delivery or prioritised decision‑making.


- **Relevant History**  
  - The Tech Backlog was introduced to capture engineering‑led work outside the product roadmap.  
  - Over time, intake became permissive and ceremonies lapsed.  
  - During busy Business  periods, the backlog increasingly functioned as storage rather than a delivery queue.


- **Domain Boundaries**  
  Data Engineering, Platform Engineering, Technical Analysts, Engineering Management


- **Assumptions Already in Place**  
  - ~10% departmental capacity was notionally allocated to Tech Backlog work.  
  - Business boards represent the department’s primary intake mechanisms.


- **Known Constraints**  
  - Limited control over JIRA configuration and ticket creation behaviour  
  - High ticket volume and historic accumulation

## 🎯 **Scope**

- **In Scope:**  
  - Analysis of Tech Backlog and department‑wide ticket behaviour  
  - Definition of intake, prioritisation, ownership, and maintenance standards  
  - Board cleanup and formalisation recommendations  

- **Out of Scope:**  
  - Delivery of individual backlog items  
  - Changes to core JIRA administration or tooling

- **Assumptions:**  
  - Behaviour visible in JIRA reflects underlying process health  
  - Improving decision hygiene will improve delivery outcomes

- **Constraints:**  
  - Admin restrictions on JIRA  
  - Legacy tickets and inconsistent historical practices

- **Non‑Goals:**  
  - Re‑prioritising the entire product roadmap  
  - Policing individual ticket authors

## 🔍 **Analysis Plan**

### **Key Questions to Resolve**
- How much work is truly waiting to be actioned?  
- Where and why does work get stuck?  
- What signals indicate poor process health?  
- Is backlog size or backlog behaviour the primary issue?  
- What standards are missing for predictability and accountability?


### **Investigation Steps**
- Define broad JQL filters to avoid bias from existing labels  
- Export board data to CSV for offline analysis  
- Perform exploratory analysis using IntelliJ and custom prompts  
- Use Augment to generate additional code and derived metrics  
- Visualise findings in Excel for clarity and comparison  
- Synthesize insights into management‑ready narratives


### **Artefacts Produced**
- CSV analysis datasets  
- Graphs and charts (age, flow, quality, duplication)  
- Slide decks (Phase 1 & Phase 2 analysis)  
- Written recommendations and process proposals  

---

## 🧩 **Architecture & Design Notes**

- **Communication Pattern Choices:**  
  Data‑led storytelling rather than policy‑led enforcement

- **Storage Decisions:**  
  CSV‑based analysis to ensure transparency and repeatability

- **Failure Modes Identified:**  
  - Permissive intake  
  - Optional ownership  
  - Parking‑lot statuses  
  - Lack of closure discipline  

- **Trade‑offs Considered:**  
  Strict upfront controls vs lightweight, enforceable standards

- **Alternatives Rejected:**  
  Further reporting without structural change to intake and ownership
