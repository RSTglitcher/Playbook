:::tip 📌 Executive Summary

**Project Purpose**
To support the migration to Datadog as the primary observability platform in order to improve visibility, resilience, and decision‑making during high‑traffic and failure scenarios, while reducing operational noise and inconsistency across services.

**Problem Statement**
Existing monitoring and logging approaches were fragmented and overly verbose, making it difficult to quickly understand system behaviour, diagnose issues during peak traffic events, and confidently prioritise remediation under pressure.

:::

## 🧭 **Context & Background**

This work took place during a broader platform migration toward Datadog, following multiple incidents where limited observability increased time‑to‑understanding and recovery effort.

- **Trigger / Catalyst**
  High‑traffic events exposed gaps in monitoring and logging, where teams struggled to distinguish actionable signals from background noise.

- **Relevant History**
  - Services produced large volumes of logs with inconsistent tagging.
  - Existing tooling made correlation between metrics, logs, and traces difficult.
  - Incident investigations relied heavily on SME interpretation rather than shared visibility.

- **Domain Boundaries Involved**
  Platform engineering, service teams, performance engineering, on‑call responders

- **Assumptions Already in Place**
  - Observability is a shared platform concern, not a per‑service afterthought.
  - Better signals improve both incident response and day‑to‑day delivery confidence.

- **Known Constraints**
  - Legacy logging practices
  - Cost considerations related to log volume and retention

## 🔍 **Analysis Plan**

### **Key Questions to Resolve**

- What information is actually needed during an incident?
- Which logs and metrics are actively used vs ignored?
- How can tagging and structure improve correlation?
- How should observability scale for peak‑traffic events?

### **Investigation Steps**

- Review existing logging and monitoring practices
- Analyse incident retrospectives to identify recurring blind spots
- Examine Datadog dashboards for clarity and consistency
- Work with engineers to identify low‑value noise
- Align on principles for signal quality and ownership

### **Artefacts Produced**

- Observability requirements and principles
- Migration and alignment documentation
- Recommendations for logging and metric hygiene

---

## 🧩 **Architecture & Design Notes**

- **Communication Pattern Choices:**
  Incident‑driven discussions rather than tooling evangelism

- **Storage and Cost Considerations:**
  Reducing unnecessary log volume where possible

- **Failure Modes Considered:**
  - Alert fatigue
  - Hidden failure signals
  - Over‑collection without insight

- **Trade‑offs Considered:**
  Granularity vs cost, completeness vs usability

- **Alternatives Rejected (and why):**
  Maintaining fragmented tooling that obscures cross‑service behaviour
