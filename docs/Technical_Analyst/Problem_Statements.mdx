:::tip Concept Overview
A **Problem Statement** defines the core issue a system, feature, or workflow is intended to solve.
For a Technical Analyst, it anchors analysis by clarifying scope, constraints, and desired outcomes before any solutioning begins.
- Establish a shared understanding of the issue at hand
- Prevent premature solutioning by focusing on *what* is wrong, not *how* to fix it
- Provide clarity for engineers, stakeholders, and decision‑makers
- Ensure alignment between business goals and technical execution
:::

---

### Principles:

- **Neutrality** – Describe the problem without implying a solution
- **Specificity** – Clearly define the symptoms, context, and impact
- **Measurability** – Identify what “resolved” looks like
- **Scope Awareness** – Avoid expanding beyond the actual issue
- **Stakeholder Alignment** – Ensure all parties agree on the definition

### Remit:

- Capturing the core issue in clear, concise language
- Identifying affected systems, users, and workflows
- Documenting assumptions, constraints, and unknowns
- Providing context for downstream analysis and design
- Ensuring the problem is framed in a way that enables objective evaluation

## 🧩 Technical Skills

<Tabs>
<TabItem value="systems" label="Systems & Architecture">

- Identify which components are involved in the problem
- Map symptoms to system boundaries and dependencies
- Distinguish between root causes and surface‑level issues
- Understand how architecture influences problem scope

</TabItem>

<TabItem value="data" label="Data Engineering">

- Recognise data quality, lineage, or transformation issues
- Understand how data flows contribute to symptoms
- Identify where inconsistencies or bottlenecks originate
- Validate assumptions using logs, metrics, and queries

</TabItem>

<TabItem value="software" label="Software Engineering">

- Understand how APIs, services, and modules interact
- Identify where logic, validation, or error handling may fail
- Recognise patterns that indicate systemic vs isolated issues
- Evaluate how design choices influence the problem

</TabItem>

<TabItem value="tools" label="Tools & Analysis">

- Use observability tools to gather evidence (logs, metrics, traces)
- Review documentation, tickets, and historical incidents
- Use CLI, SQL, or scripting to validate assumptions
- Apply structured analysis frameworks to refine the problem

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Key Strategies

<Tabs>
<TabItem value="approach" label="Analysis Approach">

1. **Clarify the context** – Who is affected? When does it occur?
2. **Describe the symptoms** – What is happening vs what should happen
3. **Identify constraints** – Technical, operational, or business limitations
4. **Separate facts from assumptions** – Validate with evidence
5. **Define success** – What does “problem resolved” look like

</TabItem>

<TabItem value="scenarios" label="Scenario Analysis">

- Compare expected vs actual behaviour
- Identify edge cases where the problem manifests
- Evaluate how the issue scales under load or volume
- Consider environmental differences (dev, staging, prod)

</TabItem>

<TabItem value="stakeholder" label="Stakeholder Alignment">

- Ensure all parties agree on the problem definition
- Highlight business impact and urgency
- Document trade-offs or constraints influencing the framing
- Provide a neutral, solution‑agnostic summary

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

---

## Questions a TA Should Ask

- “What is the exact behaviour that is incorrect or unexpected?”
- “Who is impacted, and how severely?”
- “When does the issue occur, and under what conditions?”
- “What assumptions are we making that need validation?”
- “What does success look like once this problem is resolved?”

---

## Key Outputs of a Problem Statement

- A clear, concise articulation of the issue
- Defined scope, constraints, and affected components
- Evidence supporting the problem definition
- Alignment between stakeholders and engineering
- A foundation for requirements, design, and technical analysis
