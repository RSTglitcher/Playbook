:::tip Concept Overview
Onboarding as a **Technical Analyst** is the process of building a clear, accurate mental model of a new department.
The goal is not to memorise details, but to understand **purpose, systems, workflows, data, constraints, and operational realities**.

This guide provides a structured, repeatable method for onboarding into any department, enabling a TA to become effective quickly and confidently.
:::

---

# 🧭 Onboards to any Department

A TA’s onboarding journey follows a predictable pattern:

1. **Understand the purpose**
2. **Map the systems**
3. **Trace the workflows**
4. **Understand the data**
5. **Identify constraints and risks**
6. **Learn how the team works**
7. **Form the mental model**

Each step below explains **what you’re trying to understand**, **why it matters**, **how to approach it**, and **what good looks like**.

---

## 1. Department’s Purpose

### Objective
The mission, responsibilities, and outcomes the department exists to deliver.

**Why it matters**
Purpose shapes everything: architecture, workflows, priorities, constraints, and decision‑making.
Without understanding purpose, analysis becomes disconnected from reality.

### Approach
- Review department charters, OKRs, or strategy documents
- Speak with leads about what “success” means
- Identify the domains and capabilities the team owns
- Understand how the department fits into the wider organisation

**What good looks like**
You can summarise the department’s purpose in **two sentences**, and explain how its work contributes to organisational goals.

### Output
- Purpose summary
- Map of owned domains/capabilities
- List of success measures

## 2. Map the Systems & Architecture

<Tabs>
<TabItem value="inventory" label="System Inventory">

### Objective
What systems, services, and repositories exist — and why they exist.

**Why it matters**
A TA must know the landscape before analysing behaviour or identifying risks.

### Approach
- Catalogue systems and repos
- Capture the purpose of each in one sentence
- Identify core vs supporting systems

**What good looks like**
A clear, concise inventory that explains the system landscape at a glance.

### Output
- System inventory
- Purpose summaries
- Ownership map

</TabItem>

<TabItem value="architecture" label="Architecture">

### Objective
How systems fit together and how data, events, and responsibilities flow between them.

**Why it matters**
Architecture reveals boundaries, dependencies, and potential failure points.

### Approach
- Review architecture diagrams (if they exist)
- Trace interactions between components
- Identify internal and external dependencies
- Understand integration points

**What good looks like**
A high‑level architecture diagram you can explain without hesitation.

### Output
- High‑level architecture map
- Dependency list
- Integration overview

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

---

## 3. Trace the Core Workflows

<Tabs>
<TabItem value="flows" label="End‑to‑End Flows">

### Objective
The key workflows the department supports and how work moves through the system.

**Why it matters**
Workflows reveal intent, behaviour, and operational expectations.

### Approach
- Identify primary workflows
- Understand triggers (events, schedules, requests)
- Identify inputs and outputs
- Identify consumers

**What good looks like**
You can walk someone through a workflow from trigger → processing → output.

### Output
- Workflow diagrams
- Trigger → output mapping

</TabItem>

<TabItem value="behaviour" label="Behaviour & Variants">

### Objective
How workflows behave under different conditions.

**Why it matters**
Edge cases and variants often reveal the real complexity.

### Approach
- Identify happy path vs edge cases
- Understand latency, throughput, and scaling behaviour
- Identify known failure modes

**What good looks like**
A clear understanding of how workflows behave in normal and exceptional scenarios.

### Output
- Behaviour notes
- Failure mode list

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

---

## 4. Communication Patterns

### Objective
How systems talk to each other and what guarantees those interactions rely on.

**Why it matters**
Communication patterns define reliability, coupling, and operational risk.

### Approach
- Identify integration methods (APIs, events, queues, batch jobs, file drops)
- Review data contracts and schemas
- Identify upstream and downstream systems
- Understand reliability guarantees (ordering, retries, durability)

**What good looks like**
You can explain how information moves through the system and where it might fail.

### Output
- Integration map
- Contract summaries
- Upstream/downstream list

---

## 5. Data & Domain

<Tabs>
<TabItem value="domain" label="Domain Concepts">

### Objective
The key domain objects and the rules that govern them.

**Why it matters**
Domain understanding is essential for shaping requirements and identifying problems.

### Approach
- Identify core entities
- Understand what each represents
- Identify invariants and rules
- Map state transitions

**What good looks like**
You can explain the domain in simple, accurate language.

### Output
- Domain glossary
- Entity/state diagrams

</TabItem>

<TabItem value="dataflows" label="Data Flows">

### Objective
Where data comes from, how it changes, and where it goes.

**Why it matters**
Data flows reveal dependencies, risks, and operational constraints.

### Approach
- Identify data sources
- Understand transformations
- Identify sinks and destinations
- Review validation and persistence logic

**What good looks like**
A clear understanding of how data moves through the system.

### Output
- Data flow diagrams
- Transformation notes

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

---

## 6. Operational Context

### Objective
How the system behaves in production and what operational realities shape it.

**Why it matters**
Operational context reveals constraints, risks, and priorities.

### Approach
- Review SLAs and SLOs
- Explore dashboards, alerts, and logs
- Review incident history
- Identify runbooks and procedures
- Understand performance and cost considerations

**What good looks like**
You understand how the system behaves when things go right — and wrong.

### Output
- Operational notes
- Incident patterns
- Observability overview

---

## 7. Identify Constraints & Risks

<Tabs>
<TabItem value="constraints" label="Constraints">

### Objective
The limitations that shape design and behaviour.

**Why it matters**
Constraints define what is possible, feasible, and safe.

### Approach
- Identify architectural constraints
- Identify regulatory or compliance requirements
- Identify operational constraints

**What good looks like**
A clear understanding of what cannot change — and why.

### Output
- Constraint list
- Compliance notes

</TabItem>

<TabItem value="risks" label="Risks">

### Objective
Where the system is fragile or exposed.

**Why it matters**
Risks guide prioritisation, design choices, and operational readiness.

### Approach
- Identify known risks
- Identify critical dependencies
- Identify assumptions
- Identify bottlenecks or single points of failure

**What good looks like**
A risk map that highlights where attention is needed.

### Output
- Risk register
- Dependency map

</TabItem>
</Tabs>

---

## 8. Learn the Team’s Ways of Working

### Objective
How the team collaborates, communicates, and makes decisions.

**Why it matters**
A TA must integrate into the team’s rhythm to be effective.

### Approach
- Identify roles and responsibilities
- Understand ceremonies (standups, planning, refinement, reviews)
- Understand decision‑making patterns
- Review documentation standards
- Establish communication channels

**What good looks like**
You know who to speak to, when, and for what.

### Output
- Team map
- Ceremony notes
- Communication plan
